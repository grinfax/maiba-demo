Research

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) affects 1 in 59 children in the United States and 23.8 million people worldwide. Therapy based on the principles of applied behavior analysis (ABA) has been shown to improve language, social interactions, and independent functioning of individuals with ASD. Despite its effectiveness, there remain barriers to treatment access. Behavioral interventions involve individualized, one-to-one therapeutic interactions and thus can be very costly. Given the increasing number of individuals diagnosed with ASD, there are many individuals who are not receiving treatment at a therapeutic dose because there simply aren’t enough providers. Additionally, for those who do have access to treatment, some skills learned in training context often require additional training or supports to transfer to more naturalistic learning or community environments. Immersive technologies, such as augmented and mixed reality, can alleviate these challenges. The U-Link Augmented Reality for Applied Behavior Analysis—Evaluations, Creations, and Applications team aims to integrate augmented and mixed reality technologies into ABA therapy such that intervention can be rapidly developed and delivered to people with ASD.

Our work

  • DT Love
    DTLove is an experimental learning application to evaluate and improve the efficiency of discrete trials in virtual reality.
  • Evaluation of virtual, augmented and mixed realities in scalability in autism treatment.
    We are actively evaluating the efficiencyand best use cases for virtual and augmented reality in the treatment of ASD.
  • XR for employment skills
    Our multidisciplinary research aims to mitigate these challenges to make training of employment skills affordable, reachable, and tailored to the individual’s environment.

Podcast: Addressing autism spectrum disorder
The University of Miami is at the forefront of the search for deeper understanding of autism spectrum disorder, with which one in 54 children is diagnosed. Working with partners across the University, researchers and clinicians of the Department of Psychology and Center for Autism and Related Disabilities are shedding new light on every aspect of ASD, from its biological and genetic pathways to innovative diagnostic and therapeutic approaches. At the heart of their work are the individuals and families whose needs and challenges provide the impetus to find answers as they strive to optimize the human potential of people with ASD.
